Chicago Botanic Garden

[School project] New app concept

Overview

  • Project scope: 10 weeks
  • Role: UX researcher, UX designer, and UI designer [group project working with 3 partners]
  • Product type: Mobile app
  • Tools: Pen, paper, Axure, Slack, Stormboard, Optimal Workshop

We were presented with a need for a new integrated experience via mobile app for the ongoing experience of our choosing. When researching options, I saw a gap for the Chicago Botanic Garden. While they had a website, they did not have a mobile app. Many museums and gardens are moving toward interactive app experiences that provide user involvement before, during, and after their visit. The app would educate visitors and direct them through the park with interactive self-guided tours that can be selected through the application. It would also provide aspects of augmented reality with selected exhibits.

I took a lead role with my group and guided them throughout the process for our discovery, ideation, and design. We gathered initial data through user interviews and compiled the data into an affinity diagram that supported our user flow and wireframes. These wireframes were then used in a prototype for usability testing.

After our delivery of our project, I went through and did a new iteration on the design, adding in UI styling elements.



Empathize and discover

We began our exploration by conducting contextual inquiries with 8 participants of various backgrounds.

Subjects were selected based on if they have ever been to the botanical garden or if they have any interest in visiting the botanical garden. If they have an interest in visiting a botanical garden but have never been to one, then they needed to have been to some sort of museum or exhibit to see how they generally would interact in an exhibit setting. We looked to have a variety of ages and genders to make sure there were multiple demographics represented.

The following questions were asked during each contextual inquiry:

Warm up and build rapport

  • Do you enjoy walking through exhibits?
  • When was the last time you visited the botanical garden?
  • Would you call yourself more of an indoors or outdoors individual?

General inquiry

  • Tell me about the last visit you had at the botanical garden?
  • What did you enjoy most about your last visit at the botanical garden?
  • Have you used any self-guided tours at other museums at the past?
  • If given the option of receiving physical or digital passes, what would you prefer?
  • How likely to download a company's application to streamline your experience? (Scale: Unlikely, somewhat likely, definitely)

Deep focus

  • Did any part of the botanical garden stand out to you when you last visited it?
  • Was there anything you disliked about your visit to the botanical garden? Please explain why you disliked it.
  • Have you had similar experiences in the past with other museums or sites similar to the botanic garden?
  • On your last self-guided tours, were they audio based, the content was on your mobile device, or a hybrid of the two?
  • ) If the self-guided tours were only audio based, would be less inclined to use that feature? → (follow-up) what if one-time use headphones were provided at no cost?
  • If there was a mobile app for the botanical garden, would you like that?
  • What would you expect to see on that mobile app?
  • What about before and after you are at the garden?

Retrospective

  • What would be one thing that would improve your experience at the botanical garden?
  • Is there anything else about your experience at the botanical garden that you would like to share with me?


Define

Affinity mapping

We gathered all of our observations and created an affinity map with the results. I grouped the observations based on insights, and from those insights I was able to make more broad insight clusters. These insight clusters include the following themes:

  • Enjoys seeing the plants
  • Educational benefit
  • Visit Logistics
  • Free roaming over guided tour
  • Wants help navigating
  • Wants in mobile app (usage)
  • Wants in mobile app (features)

Conceptual model and user flow diagram

I created the concept modal diagram based on the results of our affinity map. This diagram shows the possible flows the user can take as they navigate through the experience. Each step in the experience is depicted in a box, and the connection arrows display the action taken.

Card sort

I used online card sort to gather feedback on the menu layout in the app.

Users were given time to do the card sort on their own. After completing the card sort, I asked leading questions to have them describe why they placed certain items into specific categories. Also, I questioned them when they had confusion. The majority were confused on what was meant by “Email” - some believed it to be the user’s email and placed it in Profile, while others thought it was the garden’s email and put it in “Contact us”. This allowed for some discussion on clarifying certain parts of the card sort. One person suggested an “About the garden” section that encompassed details on the garden that had been lumped into a few categories. Another suggestion was “Planning your visit” where many aspects about the before and during could be housed that may not make sense to be a main nav link.

Ideate and design

Mid-fi wireframes

Based on the information collected as I defined the problems being solved for, I was able to create mid-fidelity wireframes for the Chicago Botanic Garden app concept. These wireframes showcase the many features of the app. Two main themes from the affinity diagram were that our users wanted help navigating around the garden, but they also wanted the freedom to roam at their own speed. The app meets these needs by providing a map of the garden that uses geolocation technology to pinpoint the user’s current location. Users can use the QR code scan feature to look up plants they want to learn more about without the need for a guided tour.

Test

Usability test

The wireframes were turned into a prototype that we used to perform usability tests. There were 8 participants in total, and each participant walked through two scenarios:

  • Scenario 1
    “Imagine that you are planning a trip to the Chicago Botanic Gardens for the first time. You look up info on the garden hours and location, and you may want to buy tickets ahead of time using our application. Can you show me how you would use this interface to plan your trip?”

  • Scenario 2
    “Now imagine that you’ve arrived at the park. You are at the visitors center and would like to find out how to get to the Cacti area. Can you navigate to the page that will help you get there?

    Once there, you use the QR reader to scan for more info on a plant. Using this interface, can you show me how you would do this?”

Results

We worked with 4 participants of various ages, genders, and backgrounds. In reviewing our results, we were able to gather some key takeaways:

  • 4 out of 8 users had difficulty finding or using the QR code feature - some were confused on what it did.
  • All 8 users were able to successfully identify and use the "Purchase tickets" feature
  • One user commented that the QR code should be moved to the home screen to make it easier to access.
  • At least 3 users had difficulty finding or navigating the "plan your visit" menu
  • Participants shared that this may be better marketed towards hospitals, public office spaces, or when having guests over.


Iterate and deliver

Hi-fi design

Our project scope did not call for high-fidelity designs to be created; however, as a personal project I started mapping out the design concept. The green is the Chicago Botanic Garden’s current brand color, so for the app I created 4 different shades of the lime green to be used throughout.

Besides creating a basic style guide, I looked back at the feedback received from the usability tests that were conducted. One main spot of confusion was around the QR code scanner both in terms of what it does and the placement. I moved the scanner to the landing page as was suggested, and I also renamed it to “Plant info scanner” to provide some context to its use.

Reflect

I never feel like a project is complete, and with this one I feel there is much more to explore. I’d like to see another iteration done with the design to complete the high-fidelity screens and have them tested once more. The QR scanner confusion was not the only point of feedback received from the first usability test, so I feel more iterations could be done based on the data that was received.
